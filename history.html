The History of the EU

The Origins of the EU After World War II

The origins of the European Union lie in the aftermath of World War 2. Europe was deeply scarred economically, politically and socially, and the risk of new conflicts was high. The continent was also divided by the Cold War. In this context, European countries began to seek cooperation as a way to guarantee peace, stability and recovery.
A decisive moment came on May 9 in 1950, when France proposed reconciliation with Germany through cooperation in the production of coal and steel. This proposal, known as the Schuman Declaration, is considered the foundation of European integration and is commemorated today as Europe Day.

The First European Communities

In 1957, six countries (France, Germany, Italy, Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg) signed the Treaties of Rome. These treaties established the European Economic Community (EEC), whose main objective was the creation of a common market. This market aimed to ensure the free movement of goods, services, capital and people among member states.
At the same time, the European Atomic Energy Community (Euratom) was created to coordinate civilian nuclear research and promote the peaceful use of nuclear energy.
Later, the institutions of the EEC, Euratom and the European Coal and Steel Community were merged. This institutional unification improved efficiency and simplified decision-making within the European system.

Institutional Development and Early Challenges

As integration progressed, new institutions were created. The European Commission was established to propose legislation and represent the common interest of all member states.
A significant democratic milestone was reached in 1979, when members of the European Parliament were elected for the first time by universal suffrage, giving citizens direct representation at the European level.
In 1986, the Single European Act was signed. Its primary aim was to complete the single market by removing remaining barriers to trade and improve movement within the Community.

Political Change and New Opportunities in Europe

Major geopolitical changes at the end of the 80s reshaped Europe. The fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989 and the reunification of Germany had a profound impact on European integration.
Soon after, the collapse of the Soviet Union opened new opportunities for cooperation with countries in Central and Eastern Europe. These historical developments encouraged European states to pursue deeper political and economic integration.

The Birth of the European Union

In 1992 was signed the Maastricht Treaty, that officially created the European Union. This treaty expanded EU powers and launched the process of economic and monetary union, including the plan for a common currency.
The Schengen Agreement was introduced to guarantee freedom of movement, allowing people to travel between participating countries without internal border controls.
The project of a common currency progressed, leading to the introduction of the Euro, although some countries chose not to adopt it.

Enlargement and Treaty Reforms

The European Union underwent a major enlargement with the accession of ten new countries, mainly from Central and Eastern Europe.
In 2007, the Treaty of Lisbon was signed, and entered into force in 2009, this improved institutional efficiency and decision-making processes.

Economic Crisis and Social Challenges

The global financial crisis severely affected the Eurozone, creating serious economic difficulties in several countries. This period tested solidarity among member states and led to reforms in economic governance.
Around the same time, political instability caused by events such as the Arab Spring led to increased migration toward Europe.

Migration, Brexit, and Recent Developments

In 2015 over one million migrants entered the Schengen Area, creating a major migration crisis.
This situation divided EU countries and contributed to the rise of nationalist and Eurosceptic political movements.
In 2016 the United Kingdom left the EU. After lengthy negotiations, Brexit became official on 31 January 2020, making the UK the first country to leave the Union.

The European Union Today

The European Union is a unique political and economic partnership. It is founded on shared values and continues to evolve in response to challenges such as migration, economic stability, security and international relations.
