How does the EU Work?

How the European Union Is Governed

The European Union is governed through cooperation among its 27 member countries, which work together to address issues that affect all citizens. Although member states may have different interests, they collaborate to face shared challenges and adopt common solutions.
EU governance is based on a system of institutions that share responsibilities to ensure democratic decision-making, law-making, and implementation across all member states.

The Main Governing Bodies of the EU: The European Council

The EU is governed by four main institutions, each with a specific role in the decision-making process.
The European Council is composed of the political leaders of the EU member states, its main role is to set the overall direction, priorities and goals of the European Union (without passing laws).

The Main Governing Bodies of the EU: The European Commission

The European Commission represents the interests of the EU as a whole. Its key responsibilities include:

  • Proposing new EU laws
  • Ensuring that EU legislation is properly applied in all member states
  • The Commission plays a central role in turning political priorities into concrete legislative proposals and overseeing their implementation

The Main Governing Bodies of the EU: The European Parliament

The European Parliament is the directly elected body of the European Union. EU citizens elect Members of the European Parliament every five years through elections. Together with the Council of the European Union, the Parliament:

  • Debates proposed legislation
  • Approves and passes EU laws

This ensures that EU laws are shaped through democratic representation.

The Main Governing Bodies of the EU: The Council of the European Union

The Council of the European Union is made up of ministers from the national governments of the member states. It works alongside the European Parliament to:

  • Debate
  • Amend
  • Adopt EU legislation

The Council represents the interests of national governments within the EU legislative process.

The EU Legislative Process

The EU law-making process follows a shared structure:

  1. The European Commission proposes new laws
  2. The European Parliament and the Council of the European Union debate and adopt the laws
  3. The European Commission ensures the laws are applied correctly in all member states

This system balances EU-wide interests, national interests and democratic representation.

Additional EU Institutions: The Court of Justice of the European Union

The Court of Justice of the European Union is located in Luxembourg.
Its main functions are to:

  • Interpret EU law
  • Ensure EU law is applied consistently across all member states
  • Issue judgments on issues affecting EU citizens

This guarantees legal equality and uniform application of EU legislation throughout the Union.
